Write clean, clear, easy-to-consume content. Consistently. As a trusted guide, do the hard work of thinking, clarifying, and simplifying so users don’t have to. By following these guidelines, you'll create confidence-inspiring content users can count on.

Users have jobs to do, and we create content to help them get their jobs done. How our content helps users do this is as important as the technology that makes our products go. Use these techniques to achieve actionable, straightforward content to ensure user success.


Active voice

Most of the time, use active voice. It’s more direct and often uses fewer words than passive voice. In active voice, the subject performs the action. Active voice follows the pattern subject—verb—object.

Example: I (subject) drive (verb) the car (object).

In passive voice, the subject receives the action. Passive voice follows the pattern subject—the verb “to be”—past participle—(and sometimes) object.

Example: The car (subject) is (the verb “to be”) driven (past participle) by me (object).

The car, which in the active voice is the object, is now the subject. In passive voice, who or what performs the action is often not stated.

Example: The car was driven.

##### Do

The organization admin enters the provider URL.

The secondary repository should function correctly after you switch it to primary mode.

##### Don't

The provider URL is entered.

The provider URL is entered by the organization admin.

The secondary repository should function correctly after it is switched to primary mode.

Sometimes, if the subject of the sentence is unknown or unimportant, passive voice might be necessary or better. For example, including an unimportant subject might distract from the main point or add words that just aren’t helpful.

##### Do

The steps depend on whether you do this before or after the engine is deployed.

##### Don't

The steps depend on whether you do this before or after you or a project collaborator deploys the engine.


Verb tense

Choose the verb tense carefully.

**Present**

In content types such as explanations, procedures, and references, most of the time use the present tense. It’s easier to read and often shorter than the past or future tense. It meets users in the moment, where they experience something or take action.

Situation: A redirect URI appears as the user completes a form.

##### Do

Enter the provider URL. A redirect URI appears.

##### Don't

After you enter the provider URL, a redirect will appear.

**Past**

Use it when something occurred in the past and the present tense would be confusing.

Situation: The user has completed the installation.

##### Do

Use the URL you received at the end of the installation process.

##### Don't

Use the URL you receive at the end of the installation process.

**Future**

Use it when something will occur in the future and the present tense would be confusing—and whenever it helps you achieve the right [tone](/docs/content/adapting-tone-by-situation).

Especially in content types for onboarding, such as product tours and get started UIs, the future tense can sound more human and encouraging.

Situation: The deployment will occur later.

##### Do

You’ll provide details for the cloud deployment.

##### Don't

You provide details for the cloud deployment.

The contraction “you’ll” indicates the future tense. Even if the present tense is not confusing, “you’ll” can be a better choice, because it sounds warmer and less rigid. In [Writing mechanics](/site/covalent/latest/pattern/test?subPageId=oRnxIPIaHx), see the contractions we can use.


Imperative verbs

Use imperative verbs when you’re telling or inviting users to do something. Imperative verbs are clear and brisk. They don’t need a subject, which saves at least one word.

**Note:** Previously in Ixiasoft, our practice was to use a gerund as the first word in a task topic title. Gerunds are nouns that functions as verbs, like “creating” in “creating an environment.” We have discontinued that practice. Begin the title of a task topic in Ixiasoft, or the title of any procedure no matter the tool, with an imperative verb as in “create an environment.”

  

##### Do

3\. Run the script.

##### Don't

3\. You run the script.

3\. Now you’re ready to run the script.

##### Do

**Manage network connectivity**

##### Don't

**Network connectivity managemen**t

##### Do

**Allow network connections to your environment**

##### Don't

**Allowing network connections to your environment**


No excess

Excess words reduce credibility by making content look wordy. They also make users spend more time than they should trying to move forward.

When a user scans wordy content, they might wonder “How do I know what I need is here?” So cut excess by asking these questions:

*   Do our users, or does our content, need this?
    
*   Does this convey meaning that’s not already there?
    
*   Is there a shorter way to say it?
