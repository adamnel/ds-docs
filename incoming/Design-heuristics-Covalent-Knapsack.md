# Covalent / Design heuristics

Helping teams deliver experiences that are intuitive, scalable, and trustworthy and to reinforce our core design principles

# 

Overview

Design heuristics help us assess the quality of user experiences through the lens of our principles. They serve two key purposes:

1.  To guide better decision-making during the design and development process
    
2.  To offer a consistent, principle-based way to evaluate the usability, clarity, and completeness of product experiences
    

We use them at critical checkpoints like design handoff and release review. They are not strict rules. They are directional prompts that keep us focused on delivering clear, scalable, and trustworthy experiences that serve real user needs.

### 

Our six design heuristics

Each heuristic aligns to one of our design principles. Together, they cover what it takes to build a product that feels thoughtful, consistent, and adaptable to change. Each includes questions to guide reflection and evaluation.

# 

How to apply

Apply these heuristics early and often. Use them when:

*   Kicking off a project and aligning on goals
    
*   Reviewing work before handoff
    
*   Auditing released experiences for opportunities to improve

# 

Be easy

**Design for clarity, not complexity**  
Simplify content and interactions and enable collaboration between AI and humans by focusing on usability and guiding users through the easiest paths to get a job done.

### Ask yourself:

**Intuitiveness**

**Task efficiency**  

Is the feature easy to understand and use without extensive documentation?

Does the feature minimize the number of steps required to complete a task?

Does the system adapt workflows, settings, or recommendations based on user behavior and history?

Does it provide recommendations, onboarding hints, or step-by-step guidance?

Are error messages clear and does the feature provide recovery paths?

Are steps clearly connected through text that focus on helping the user complete the task confidently and efficiently?

Is the user flow clearly connected by the copy, resulting in a cohesive experience?

  

  

# 

Be scalable

Gain leverage by designing cohesive interfaces that can grow with our users, within our ecosystem, and with our industry. There should be no isolated features, functionality, or experiences.

### Ask yourself:

**Consistency across the product**

**Future-proofing and extensibility** 

Does it align with other features in terms of behavior and UI patterns?

Can the feature grow and evolve with minimal redesign?

Can the feature handle large-scale data and concurrent users efficiently?

Can users customize or extend the feature (APIs, plugin support)?

Does the naming and labeling system reflect the product’s mental model and hierarchy consistently?

Are familiar terms and content structures reusable rather than reinvented?

# 

Be trustworthy

Promote clarity, inclusivity, and accessibility to foster trust and transparency in decision making.

### Ask yourself:

**Intuitiveness**

**Security and compliance**  

Does the feature make its processing and outputs transparent, such as data transformations, logic, or decision factors (model lineage, confidence scores)?

Does it follow industry best practices for security (e.g., role-based access, encryption)?

Is the language aligned with Teradata’s product voice, clear, and inclusive, helping all users understand what’s happening, what’s expected, and what their options are ( errors, onboarding, empty states)?

Are users clearly informed about data collection and options for configuring roles and access?

Are learning resources (help docs, tutorials) easily accessible within the feature?

Does the content and design support WCAG 2.1 standards (keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, etc.)?

Does the feature explain AI-driven outputs, such as how a recommendation was made, what data was used, the model lineage, or the confidence behind a result?

  

# 

Be delightful

Encourage bold and creative solutions in the design and implementation of the UX.

### Ask yourself:

**Design aesthetics and engagement**

**Reduction of cognitive load**  

Is it visually appealing and well-structured?

  

Can users visually explore and manipulate data with ease?

Does the feature make complex tasks engaging?

Is information grouped and presented in a way that reduces decision fatigue and mental effort (progressive disclosure from UI to in-product help to docs)?

Does the tone, through copy, illustrations, and animations, engage the user while staying focused on the user needs?

Are steps clearly connected through text that focus on helping the user complete the task confidently and efficiently?

Does the experience create meaningful moments of truth, where the copy acknowledges the user’s work and highlights the business impact they’ve achieved?

  

# 

Be informed

Design with continuous learning from user interactions and behaviors.

### Ask yourself:

**Key results and feedback loops**

**Documentation and support**  

Can users provide feedback on the feature and its supporting documentation, and is it used for iteration?

Are learning resources (help docs, tutorials) easily accessible within the feature?

Does the feature have OKRs and Pendo set up to track its success?

  

Is the proposed design and content evidence-backed (e.g. has it been researched or user tested)?

# 

Be open and connected

Design with interoperability and integration in mind.

### Ask yourself:

**Standardization and open formats, cross-platform**

**Ecosystem integration**  

Does it support industry standards for interoperability (SQL, JSON, Parquet)?

  

Does it expose APIs for data exchange with other tools and services?

Does the design maintain consistency across browsers, devices, platforms and deployment types (e.g. on-prem, cloud service providers)?

Does it seamlessly connect with partner offerings, AI tools, and third-party services?
